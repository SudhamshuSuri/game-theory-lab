# Sovereign: A Game of Strategy

**The game that teaches game theory through play.**

---

## 1. Elevator Pitch

You are the ruler of a small kingdom in a world of city-states. Every decision—ally or betray, tax or invest, trust or verify—has consequences. Through 60+ scenarios, you will rediscover game theory from first principles, not because a textbook told you, but because bad strategies got your people killed. By the end, you will design the laws, markets, and incentives for your own society and watch AI agents adapt.

---

## 2. Design Philosophy

### 2.1 Gameplay First

- Never interrupt gameplay for lectures.
- All explanations happen *after* the player has experienced the concept.
- The "aha" moment is the core mechanic.

### 2.2 Discovery Over Explanation

Bad: "This is the Prisoner's Dilemma. Here is the payoff matrix."

Good: Player proposes a trade alliance. Partner betrays them. Player loses everything. Next time, Player demands hostages. Partner agrees. Both profit. Two scenarios later, the game reveals: "Congratulations. You just invented the Prisoner's Dilemma."

### 2.3 One Concept Per Scenario

- Each scenario teaches exactly one major strategic principle.
- Later scenarios combine previous principles.
- The difficulty curve is: discover → name → apply → combine.

### 2.4 Failure is Educational

- Every loss carries information about what went wrong.
- The game provides a "Why You Lost" analysis after each scenario.
- Randomness is minimized and always shown transparently.

### 2.5 Emergence Beats Scripting

- AI agents have personalities, not scripts.
- No two playthroughs are identical.
- The scenario editor allows players to create and share scenarios.

## 3. Setting: The Shattered Coast

After the Collapse of the Old Empire, the coast fractured into a dozen city-states. Each is independent, resourceful, and suspicious. You are the ruler of **Haven**, a modest port city with ambitions.

### 3.1 Factions

| Faction | Personality | Values |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| Haven (You) | Player-driven | Survival → Prosperity → Dominance |
| The Iron Guild | Greedy, Pragmatic | Profit above all |
| Freeport | Always Cooperates | Trust, Community |
| The Dominion | Always Defects | Power, Expansion |
| Stonehold | Tit-for-Tat | Honor, Reciprocity |
| The Veil | Deceptive | Information, Secrecy |
| The Covenant | Coalition-Former | Unity, Collective Good |
| Ash Collective | Grim Trigger | Justice, Retribution |
| The Fractured | Random | Chaos, Surprise |

### 3.2 Resources

- **Gold** - Universal currency for trade, hiring, construction
- **Food** - Required for population; scarcity causes unrest
- **Population** - Labor force and military recruits
- **Military** - Defense and conquest capability
- **Influence** - Soft power for diplomacy and manipulation
- **Knowledge** - Research, intelligence, and technology
- **Trust** - A resource with each faction, built by keeping promises
- **Reputation** - Global standing affecting all interactions

## 4. Learning Progression (60+ Scenarios)

The game is divided into 6 eras, each teaching a family of concepts.

### Era I: Foundations (Scenarios 1–10)

*Core concepts of strategic decision-making under scarcity.*

| # | Scenario | Concept | Setting |
|---|----------|---------|---------|
| 1 | The Empty Granary | Scarcity, opportunity cost | A harsh winter leaves your granary half-full. You must allocate food between the army, the granary for next year, and trade goods. Every choice has a trade-off. |
| 2 | The Border Dispute | Zero-sum thinking | A fertile valley lies between you and a neighbor. Only one can control it. Any gain for them is a loss for you. |
| 3 | The Wandering Merchant | Gains from trade, positive-sum | A merchant offers a deal you don't fully understand. Accepting creates value for both. Rejecting leaves both as they were. |
| 4 | The Pirate's Offer | Prisoner's Dilemma | An ally proposes a joint raid. You can honor the split or betray. Both honor = both win. Both betray = both lose. One betrays = they win it all. |
| 5 | The Market Square | Competition, dominant strategy | Two bakers in the same square must price their bread. Undercutting captures the market. Colluding maximizes joint profit. |
| 6 | The Border Again | Iterated zero-sum | Same as #2, but repeated. The player learns that repeated interaction changes optimal strategy. |
| 7 | The Alliance | Cooperation, mutual benefit | Two kingdoms both benefit from alliance, but each fears the other will not contribute equally. |
| 8 | The Fisherman's Dilemma | Tragedy of the commons | A shared lake is being overfished. Each fisherman maximizes their catch, but collectively they destroy the resource. |
| 9 | The Toll Bridge | Coordination game | Two villages must choose which bridge to repair. They both prefer the same choice. The cost is being alone. |
| 10 | The Harvest Festival | Public goods | A festival benefits everyone but costs the organizer. Everyone benefits if someone organizes, but no one wants to pay. |

**Era I Unlock:** The player receives their first "Discovery Card" — **Prisoner's Dilemma** — explaining what they've been experiencing.

### Era II: Repetition & Reputation (Scenarios 11–20)

*How repeated interactions change strategy.*

| # | Scenario | Concept | Setting |
|---|----------|---------|---------|
| 11 | The Trade Route (Repeated PD) | Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma | The same trade partner, many rounds. Past choices matter. |
| 12 | The Caravan Guard | Tit-for-Tat | A neighboring kingdom offers a mutual protection pact. Player discovers that mirroring works best. |
| 13 | The Exiled Knight | Grim Trigger | A single betrayal ends all cooperation forever. The player learns why some violations are unforgivable. |
| 14 | The Merchant's Ledger | Reputation, shadow of the future | Word spreads of your deals. A bad reputation closes doors. A good one opens them. |
| 15 | The Wedding Pact | Credible commitment | A marriage alliance requires both sides to commit. How do you make promises believable? |
| 16 | The Hostage Exchange | Commitment devices | Exchanging hostages makes betrayal costly. The player invents commitment devices. |
| 17 | The Mercenary Contract | Principal-agent problem | You hire mercenaries, but they don't fight as hard as your own soldiers. Incentives are misaligned. |
| 18 | The Tax Farmer | Moral hazard | A tax farmer pays you upfront for the right to collect taxes. He overtaxes the people because you already got paid. |
| 19 | The Royal Surveyor | Adverse selection, screening | You need a loyal advisor. Liars claim loyalty louder than the loyal. How do you filter? |
| 20 | The Siege | Commitment problems | You promise not to attack if the gates open. They don't believe you. How do you make them believe? |

**Era II Unlock:** "Discovery Card" — **Tit-for-Tat**, **Grim Trigger**, **Credible Commitment**, **Principal-Agent Problem**.

### Era III: Uncertainty & Information (Scenarios 21–30)

*Strategic decision-making with incomplete information.*

| # | Scenario | Concept | Setting |
|---|----------|---------|---------|
| 21 | The Hidden Scouts | Bayesian games, hidden information | Is the neighboring army strong or weak? You must decide based on imperfect intelligence. |
| 22 | The Fool's Gold | Signaling, costly signals | A rival claims prosperity. Is it real or a bluff? Expensive signals are more believable. |
| 23 | The Merchant's Secret | Screening | You need a business partner who won't cheat. Design a contract that only honest partners accept. |
| 24 | The Duel | Chicken | Two armies march toward each other. The first to swerve loses face. The one who doesn't dies. |
| 25 | The Bridge of Khazad | Battle of the sexes | Two allies disagree on strategy. Both prefer coordination, but each prefers a different coordinated outcome. |
| 26 | The Empty Throne | Succession game, jockeying | The king dies without heir. Multiple claimants vie for the throne. Coalition formation begins. |
| 27 | The Oracle's Riddle | Common knowledge | Everyone knows something, but does everyone know that everyone knows? Levels of shared knowledge matter. |
| 28 | The Spreading Rumor | Information cascades | A rumor spreads. People believe not what's true, but what they hear others believing. |
| 29 | The Trade Embargo | Coalition formation, voting | A vote to embargo a rival. You need 3 of 5 votes. Coalitions form and shift. |
| 30 | The Whispers | Cheap talk | Communication without cost. Can words alone create cooperation? (Usually not, but sometimes yes.) |

**Era III Unlock:** "Discovery Cards" — **Bayesian Games**, **Signaling**, **Screening**, **Chicken**, **Cheap Talk**.

### Era IV: Markets & Mechanism (Scenarios 31–40)

*Market design, auctions, and incentive engineering.*

| # | Scenario | Concept | Setting |
|---|----------|---------|---------|
| 31 | The Spice Auction | English auction | A rare spice shipment is auctioned. Price climbs. Winner's curse looms. |
| 32 | The Mining Rights | Dutch auction | The price drops until someone claims it. When do you buy? |
| 33 | The Silent Bids | First-price sealed-bid | Everyone bids secretly. Highest wins, pays their bid. How much to shade? |
| 34 | The Treasury Bonds | Vickrey auction | Highest bid wins but pays second-highest price. Truthful bidding becomes dominant. |
| 35 | The Council Election | Voting systems (plurality) | Simple plurality voting. Strategic voting emerges. Vote for your true favorite or the lesser evil? |
| 36 | The Reform Vote | Voting systems (ranked choice) | Ranked-choice voting. Does it change strategy? |
| 37 | The Guild Charter | Mechanism design | You can design the rules of the guild. What rules create the behavior you want? |
| 38 | The Royal Mint | Public goods provision | Funding the army benefits everyone. How do you collect contributions without free riders? |
| 39 | The Bad Road | Congestion game | Two routes to the market. Everyone takes the short one. Now it's congested and slower than the long one. |
| 40 | The New Trade Route | Network effects | A trade route becomes more valuable as more use it. Early adopters vs late joiners. |

**Era IV Unlock:** "Discovery Cards" — **Auctions (4 types)**, **Voting Systems**, **Mechanism Design**, **Congestion**, **Network Effects**.

### Era V: Society & Evolution (Scenarios 41–50)

*How strategies spread, evolve, and shape societies.*

| # | Scenario | Concept | Setting |
|---|----------|---------|---------|
| 41 | The Tournament | Evolutionary game theory | Submit a strategy. It faces all others in a round-robin. Which strategies survive? |
| 42 | The Replicator | Evolutionary stable strategies | Strategies that outperform spread. The population evolves. |
| 43 | The Invasion | Mutant strategy | A new strategy appears. Can it invade the existing population? |
| 44 | The Three Kingdoms | Coalition game theory | Three powers, shifting alliances. Any two can defeat the third. |
| 45 | The Great Council | Mechanism design, constitution | Design the constitution for a new alliance. You are designing incentives for future generations. |
| 46 | The Prison Without Walls | Crime & punishment | Crime exists. You can punish, but punishment is costly. Optimal deterrence theory. |
| 47 | The Charity Race | Altruism, warm-glow giving | Donating feels good. But does it change strategic dynamics? |
| 48 | The Nomads vs Settlers | Conflict, property rights | Nomads and settlers both need the land. Different concepts of ownership cause tension. |
| 49 | The Plague | Cooperation under threat | A plague threatens all. Self-isolation saves you but dooms others. Mutual isolation saves everyone. |
| 50 | The Memorial | Common-pool resource management | The fishing lake again. But now you can design the management system. Compare with #8. |

**Era V Unlock:** "Discovery Cards" — **Evolutionary Game Theory**, **ESS**, **Coalition Game Theory**, **Mechanism Design II**.

### Era VI: Mastery (Scenarios 51–60+)

*Synthesis, real-world applications, and society design.*

| # | Scenario | Concept | Setting |
|---|----------|---------|---------|
| 51 | The Salary Negotiation | Bargaining, ultimatum game | Negotiate your advisor's salary. Offer too little and they leave. Too much and treasury suffers. |
| 52 | The Merger | Bargaining with outside options | Two guilds consider merging. Each has alternatives. The best alternative sets the floor. |
| 53 | The Treaty of Freeport | Multilateral negotiation N-player | Multiple parties, multiple issues. Logrolling and package deals. |
| 54 | The Great Game | International diplomacy | A grand strategic simulation with multiple kingdoms. Balance of power. |
| 55 | The Dating Market | Matching theory, stable marriage | Pairing problem. Can you find stable matches? |
| 56 | The Algorithm's Choice | AI alignment, principal-agent with AI | You design an AI agent's objective function. It optimizes. The results are unexpected. |
| 57 | The Distributed Ledger | Blockchain, consensus mechanisms | Design a consensus protocol. Trade off security, speed, and decentralization. |
| 58 | The Cloud Wars | Computing resource allocation | Multiple services share computing resources. Peak demand causes congestion. Dynamic pricing emerges. |
| 59 | The Social Network | Network games, platform design | Design a social network. Attention is the resource. Engagement vs well-being trade-off. |
| 60 | The Society | Capstone: design your own society | All tools unlocked. Design laws, taxes, markets, punishments, rewards. Run for 100 turns. AI agents adapt. |

**Era VI Unlock:** All remaining "Discovery Cards". Player receives a **Certificate of Strategic Mastery**.

## 6. AI Architecture

### 6.1 Personality System

Each AI agent has a personality defined by 7 traits (1–10):

| Trait | Low (1-3) | Medium (4-7) | High (8-10) |
|-------|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Greed | Selfless | Fair | Greedy |
| Trust | Suspicious | Cautious | Trusting |
| Revenge | Forgiving | Measured | Vengeful |
| Risk | Risk-averse | Balanced | Risk-seeking |
| Planning | Myopic | Medium-term | Long-term |
| Deception | Honest | Opaque | Deceptive |
| Reciprocity | Ignores others | Conditional | Strong reciprocity |

### 6.2 Strategy Selection

AI selects strategy through a weighted function:
1. Base weight from personality traits
2. Modify weight based on past interactions with player
3. Modify weight based on scenario context
4. Add noise (randomness) proportional to agent's unpredictability trait
5. Select strategy via weighted random

### 6.3 AI Types (Personality Presets)

```javascript

  alwaysCooperate:   { greed:1, trust:10, revenge:1, risk:3, planning:5, deception:1, reciprocity:8 },
  alwaysDefect:      { greed:10, trust:1, revenge:5, risk:7, planning:3, deception:6, reciprocity:1 },
  titForTat:         { greed:3, trust:5, revenge:8, risk:4, planning:6, deception:2, reciprocity:10 },
  random:            { /* randomized each interaction */ },
  greedy:            { greed:10, trust:2, revenge:3, risk:8, planning:2, deception:7, reciprocity:2 },
  riskSeeking:       { greed:7, trust:3, revenge:4, risk:10, planning:2, deception:5, reciprocity:3 },
  riskAverse:        { greed:3, trust:6, revenge:4, risk:1, planning:8, deception:3, reciprocity:6 },
  longTermPlanner:   { greed:4, trust:5, revenge:5, risk:3, planning:10, deception:4, reciprocity:7 },
  revengeDriven:     { greed:3, trust:2, revenge:10, risk:6, planning:2, deception:3, reciprocity:5 },
  trustBuilder:      { greed:2, trust:7, revenge:3, risk:3, planning:8, deception:1, reciprocity:9 },
  opportunist:       { greed:8, trust:3, revenge:4, risk:8, planning:5, deception:8, reciprocity:4 },
  coalitionFormer:   { greed:5, trust:6, revenge:4, risk:5, planning:7, deception:5, reciprocity:8 },
  deceptive:         { greed:7, trust:2, revenge:5, risk:6, planning:6, deception:10, reciprocity:3 },
  learningAgent:     { /* adapts based on player behavior */ },

```

### 6.4 Learning Agent

The learning agent maintains:
- A history of all interactions with the player
- A model of the player's likely choices
- The ability to update its model after each interaction
- Multiple models (always cooperate best response, tit-for-tat, grim trigger)
- Chooses the model that would have performed best historically

## 10. Balancing Strategy

### 10.1 Difficulty Curve

- Early scenarios: obvious choices that fail in unexpected ways
- Mid scenarios: no dominant strategy, trade-offs are painful
- Late scenarios: optimal play requires combining multiple concepts
- Sandbox: no constraints, emergent complexity

### 10.2 Payoff Design Principles

1. Every payoff matrix must have a clear "lesson"
2. Dominant strategies exist only in scenarios teaching dominance
3. Mixed strategy equilibria have no pure dominant choice
4. Payoffs are scaled to player's current resource levels
5. AI always plays rationally within its personality constraints

### 10.3 Avoiding Frustration

- First attempt at a scenario is low-stakes
- Resource penalties never cause game-over (they create interesting problems)
- "Why You Lost" is detailed and constructive
- Player can retry any scenario with knowledge of AI personality
- Hint system: player can ask "What am I missing?" once per scenario

## 11. Educational Features

### 11.1 Replay Mode

Watch any completed scenario play out step-by-step. See what each agent knew and chose at each decision point. Overlay the payoff matrix.

### 11.2 Timeline Viewer

Scroll through your entire game history. See how relationships, resources, and strategy evolved. Filter by concept, faction, or outcome type.

### 11.3 Strategy Comparison

Compare two scenarios side by side. See how changing one variable (e.g., making it a repeated game) changes optimal strategy.

### 11.4 Heatmaps

Visual representation of your decisions. Which choices do you over/under-use? Where are your blind spots?

### 11.5 Decision Trees

For any scenario, view the complete decision tree. What would have happened if you chose differently?

### 11.6 AI Reasoning Viewer

After a scenario concludes, see exactly what each AI agent was thinking: their personality analysis, what they predicted you would do, and why they chose what they did.

### 11.7 Sandbox Mode

After completing Era III, sandbox unlocks. Choose factions, set their personalities, design payoff matrices, and run simulations. See what strategies emerge.

### 11.8 Scenario Editor

Create custom scenarios. Define the setting, characters, choices, AI personalities, and payoff matrices. Share with the community.

### 11.9 Challenge Mode

Weekly challenges with constraints (e.g., "Win this scenario without using Tit-for-Tat"). Leaderboards.

### 11.10 Daily Puzzle

A single scenario with a unique twist. One chance to solve it. Compare your answer with the community.

### 11.11 Infinite Procedural Scenarios

Using template permutations, generate infinite scenarios that teach the same concepts with different framing.

## 12. Analytics & Learning Measurement

### 12.1 What We Measure

| Metric | How | Purpose |
|--------|-----|---------|
| Scenario retries | Count of attempts per scenario | Identify which concepts are hard |
| Decision patterns | Cluster analysis of choices | Measure strategic sophistication |
| Time per scenario | Duration | Gauge engagement vs confusion |
| Discovery retention | Post-discovery scenario performance | Verify learning transfer |
| Transfer test | Framing-shifted scenarios | Test abstract understanding |
| AI prediction accuracy | Player's ability to predict AI choices | Measure strategic empathy |
| Sandbox complexity | What players build in sandbox | Measure synthesis ability |

### 12.2 Validation

- Pre/post test: Players take a game theory quiz before and after playing
- Transfer tasks: Players are given real-world strategic scenarios and asked to analyze them
- Longitudinal: Re-test after 1 month, 6 months to measure retention
- Comparison: Compare with textbook learners on identical tests

## 15. Technical Requirements

- **Platform:** Web (primary), desktop export (secondary), mobile (tertiary)
- **Framework:** Vanilla JS with modular architecture
- **Storage:** LocalStorage + IndexedDB for larger histories
- **Rendering:** DOM-based with CSS animations, Canvas for visualizations
- **Testing:** Jest/Vitest for unit tests, Playwright for E2E
- **Build:** Vite for development and production builds
- **Analytics:** Privacy-focused, self-hosted or optional
